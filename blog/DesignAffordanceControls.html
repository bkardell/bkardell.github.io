Design Afforance Controls

As I've been working on OpenUI, I have had broader and deeper looks across several UI platforms and their histories. I think that in this I've recently had something of a "deeper realization", something I don't think entirely 'clicked' in my head until now, but I think should potentially shape a lot discussions. In this post I'll attempt to articulate it.

The HTML specification describes the control created by <details> and <summary> as a 'disclosure widget'. The particulars of how it is defined, I'd like to argue, came from a long line of attempting to reasoning about all 'widgets' (interactive controls) the same way. But... are they?

Is a 'disclosure widget' exactly like a <select> control? I think that the answer is "no". There is something fundamentally different about these two things that require us to think about them a little differently. I'd like to offer the following as food for thought:

I believe there are a broad class of "controls" (several defined by ARIA even) whose very essence might be described as to "provide design components which allow authors to provide user affordances that improve the users ability to consume the content within a particular design.". I am going to label these here as "Design Affordance Controls".

What's the difference?

Key to understanding why believe that such a distinction matters are the words 'design' 'affordances' and, especially, 'content'.

Controls like <select> (nearly all controls in HTML so far) define a fundamental requirement of interaction. Their primary purpose is to collect, and without this they aren't especially useful.

However, if you take a step back, it seems that the same is not true of seveal other controls: 'disclosure widgets', tabs, accordions, and trees (what I am calling "Design Affordance Controls"). They are, first and foremost, natural document content.. One could provide all of the content contained by these 'widgets' in perfectly meaningful and coherent content and logical document order without any design or interaction affordances at all (save the UA stylesheet), and what would be lost is mainly just 'pleasantness' of consumption.

Fundamentally, any of these could be provided as sections with headings and content which might have affordances. Here, interaction is secondary and deployed by authors in order to provide helpful affordances for users to be able to more easily consume the content.

But, even more intrestingly: This is done as part of a particular design. The sections below, I hope, help to illustrate and highlight the difference.

The print test

Perhaps the simplest example to illustrate this is to consider: What should happen if you printed the document?

While it's unsurprising that controls whose nature is 'collection of input' like <select> print _as a control_ is that uniformly true among all controls?

<details> and <summary> are designed with similar philosophy. They were designed as if "control-ness" and "hidden-ness" were somehow fundamental. What is printed is the control, in its current state - period. We cannot choose to provide styles which do otherwise.

Imagine that I buit a a site about receipes. I may choose to arrange contents of each receipe into tabs grouped as 'ingredients', 'instructions' and 'dietary information' in order to provide a set of affordances for a user to consume them more easily. But really, it's just that: A convenience of design affordanced. If I printed it, don't I actually want to at least have the ability to print all of that content?

The same sort of "test" kind of applies to reader mode too: What should happen? Our current not-great answers, which frequently lose content are based on how we treat "control-ness" and "hidden-ness" as fundamental.

The "PE test"

Imagine you wanted some kind of collapsible content, and you're sending CSS, HTML and JavaScript down to the browser. If you send the content marked up "as a control" already assuming its state and all affordances in the markup, you've left a possibility for real problems.

Any of a myriad of problems can get in the way and leave your user in one of two bad states:

  • (ideally) a UI which seems to imply that a section could be collapsed, but frustratingly that won't seem to work.
  • (much worse) a UI which seems to imply there is content (which their is) which the user should be able to expand, but frustratingly cannot.

A more robust solution would deliver the content as content and enhance good and meaningful content with affordances if that was possible. In fact, this is precisely what several of the original PE examples/essays did. The essence of the element didn't change, only the afforances inside it did, and only if they could. They were perfectly good on their own and were careful to avoid the above kind of situation.

I think that with time we've shown there's even more to it for some controls (my "Design Affordance Controls"): It's not only about whether that is "possible", but also whether it is desirable - and that is often subject to design choices. We've already mentioned print, but let's dig into another...

"Selectively is or isn't a control"

If your browser window is big enough, the left side of this website (as of this writing, at least) is a bunch of information about me. It's not, very probably, why you're here. You didn't come to learn about me, you want to read the <main> content. But, that's not a design problem either... In fact, I think that showing you both and making it easy for you to get that information without being overly distracting is a feature of the design.

However, in a smaller viewport, this would become really inconvenient for a user. It would involve scrolling through a lot of "noise" in order to get to the actual content. The "easy" solution might be to just hide it. However, as I said, making it easy to find that information is a feature. So, on a small screen I choose to put a disclosure widget that is collapsed by default with 'author information' in the summary.

Almost every website on earth, it seems, has some "spiritually similar" idea (hamburger menus, for example). Cool...

Except... wait... is it? As I said, on a small screen that is very clearly a 'disclosure widget', but this isn't a thing you can do 'out of the box' with a 'disclosure widget' as defined. It's exactly the same content - but that's not how its API surface of <details> and <summary> work. At all.

It seems that in neither print nor websites can I say "it's just good content and not a control at all" because it is designed as if, like a <select> its "control-ness" is fundamental... But... is it? Or are these affordances I choose to add to content based on how my user is consuming it? Lots of common examples like this would seem to point to the latter.

Linking and Searching

Pages allow authors to share links to anchors and, in the future, perhaps anything that could be identified as 'anchor like'. Find in page allows authors to find content in the page by searching. Today, this is a problem for <details><summary>, in part because of how fundamental 'controlness' is baked in and it will be for other controls of this class as well if we continue to think of them this way.

The fact that this is an evident pain in many of these kinds of controls seems to further point to their 'contentness' and that their UI affordances are secondary and not fundamental.

Interchangability?

Because they are about design, there is a level of interchangeability here that feels kind of important to think about. There isn't a "right" answer to which you should use and we've shown (I hope) that it might be useful to go at least from non-interactive, meaningful content to interactive afforances mixed in, and back even. But... Might it be desirable to even change which affordances are mixed in?

"Responsive Tabs" help illustrate that there are at least reasons that aren't just out of left field to wonder. They force us to ask some new, hard questions. They also help illustrate that sometimes (especially over time) the lines between "what is the difference between two of them" can even get more than a little blurry.

For example: Many people would describe that responsive tabs "turn into" an accordion control... But, what even is an accordion control?

It seems like this should have a relatively straightforward answer, however, if I'm honest, it seems to me the "true" answer is: "We kind of don't really know".

We don't have a definive, simple, nearly universal or long lasting definition. Even ARIA's accordion has something of an identity crisis not entirely unlike the one describe by Monica Dinculescu in her excellent talk Input I <3 you, but you're bringing me down..

Because of how and when it was defined in history, it is defined very broadly. This creates severe challenges because so many questions about how "it" should work are fundamentally based on first having more specific answers.

The ARIA Practices Guide (APG) itself has struggled with this. If you start thinking about accordions as "single select, where one is always open" you will be led inexorably to one set of answers about keyboard handling, roles, sequential tab order, etc. This is how APG and examples were previously. However, if you think about it terms of a thing where you can you open 0 to N of them, it will be just the opposite. This is how APG seems to be moving now.

How this shapes the way we look at things

I expect, a lot. If we approached designing this class of controls differently, with these things in mind we'd design something very different than without them. If we didn't have <details> and <summary> today, for example, how would we create it? I expect differently if we took this view and asked different questions. I wish I could say exactly how differently, but I can say that it would probably involve not changing the fundamental 'ness' of otherwise good, meaningful content and not having all of these challenges.

If <details> and <summary> wasn't supported by the browser, the content would run together (unknown elements are approximately span) and the summary didn't provide some kind of accessible labelling for the content. You could, of course, work around it, but thinking about it this way seems to help create a better rollout story too.

Thinking about these questions prompts us to ask new questions - like is one kind of "accordion" really just another kind of tabs? The the other one really just a group of disclosure widgets? What is the impact of "changing" affordances (or even roles) like this, if we could design something?

In truth, it seems that we lack good studies on some pretty fundamental things here, and I feel like part of why that is is because of how we've thought about all controls the same way. New questions would be necessarily asked by shifting our thinking. It's worth thinking about things like 'responsive tabs' and considering what has changed? and why does that matter? in order to inform questions about things like accordions. If these were elements, what would their DOM API and IDL look like? How would they cope with these things? Could they? This is a thing that could use serious R&D and study.
