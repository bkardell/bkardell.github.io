W3C Challenges

I'd like to talk about a particularly hard problem at W3C.

Background

Back in 2013 or so, a group of us began efforts fo reform a lot of stuff about the way standards worked and the way that we think about them. At the W3C, part of this strategy dealt with getting people elected to the two elected bodies within the W3C that help drive the overall process and focus. If you're unfamilliar with how the W3C works, here's a short (~400 words) Primer for Busy People.

Since then, we have elected a lot of good people and I'm pleased to say that there has been a lot of positive change. It's been moderately more diverse too, but... we can do better.

A Discussion

Every year, many of the members of W3C meet up for an event called TPAC (which is short for Technical Plenary / Advisory Committee). During this time, lots of groups meet face-to-face to do a lot of concentrated work, the Advisory Committe hears/discusses proposed things and, generally there's a number of presentations too. But maybe the best thing about TPAC is "breakout sessions". Effectively, at least one day is reserved for the attendees themselves to establish topics that interest a lot of people and meet and discuss it. This year, two of my friends Sangwahn Moon and Natasha Rooney (who serve on the TAG and AB respectively) led a breakout session about TAG and AB, encouraging more people (and more diverse people) to run for these positions.

This thrilled me because, it's full of some really hard problems that I've been thinking about for a long time and I'm unsure how to address well. Both in the breakout session, and afterward, there was a lot of interesting discussion about what these challenges are.

Sizing ACs do the nominating and they can nominate only one person, and only one person per organization can be serve at once that much is written down.
"Historical Systemic" Biases

There's one class of challenge that I will call "Historically Systemic". There's nothing "written down" that necessitates this problem, it's just sort of how it's always worked because it naturally flows out of how things are organized. Here's what I mean: Currently, there's only about 470 ACs in the whole world. Historically, only those who I will call "an involved AC" (meaning, someone who spends significant time playing this role and, for example, votes) will actually consider nominating. Surpringly, by far, the vast majority don't even vote. Voting is, technically easier than actively nominating, so the number of people who do that is smaller still. Let's just take an educated guess and say that "only about 50 ACs actually ever nominate". Wow, that's a really small group. It also turns out to be, itself, kind of vanilla.

Generally speaking, the possible people they would historically nominate are the pool of people in their own organization. Practically speaking, there's no point in nominating someone unless they can win, which historically meant asking the question "will enough people recognize this person and their work in standards".

Now, here's a relatively simple observation: A number of organization belongs "passively" to one or two working groups and only have 1 or 2 people on those groups. Bigger, wealthier organizations more directly invested in the web (for example, browsers) tend to have far more people involved in standards. Here's a quick way understand this point: The CSSWG is one of the bigger and more inclusive working groups.
