W3C Elections Just Got A Whole Lot Harder

There's a worrying new element to W3C politics and I think we need to pitch in and help...

Who isn't the only question anymore

So, let's just get the simple stuff out of the way: There are 2 seats up for reelction in the W3C TAG. If you're not familliar with how the W3C Works or what the TAG is or why it matters, fear not, there's a ~400 word Primer for Busy People. There are 4 nominations for these seats, 2 of them are incumbents. Both of these incumbents (David Baron and Andrew Betts) haven't been there long and they've both done an excellent job so far. You can read their statements for yourself, but David and Andrew are who I support this go round.

Sadly, who to vote for isn't the only question anymore - it's now also how to vote and this is new, likely to confuse and has potentially bad outcomes unless we can really help people be aware of how to prevent outcomes they don't want.

The Change

Until recently, the W3C used a simple plurality voting system to elect for TAG and AB. Each member could cast one vote per available open seat and then they'd simply count up the votes for each candidate and the ones with the highest totals win. If you live in a country (like the US) that uses a similar form then you are well aware of the unfortunate mental game that plays out in your head: Imagine that there are 3 candidates A, B and C. You really, really like Candidate A, and you really, really dislike Candidate C. Your "honest" vote should be for Candidate A. However, because this system is so simple, it's also very easy to see the bad outcome that's possible. If you aren't entirely sure that Candidate A can win, then a vote for them actually helps Candidate C by not increasing the competitive count of Candidate B. Real honesty can "spoil" the vote, leading toward your least desirable outcome because the system itself doesn't allow you to clearly describe your intent or preference.

There are many other voting systems that aim to allow votes to be more expressive, and that's generally a good thing. The W3C recently changed how voting works to use one of these forms. Again, this seems generally like a positive idea. However, there are many variants and many implications. Given what seem to be basically the same inputs different systems and wind up with very different results. This voting system is pretty complicated, but I'm embedding a pretty good video here that is pretty short and makes the general concept clear..

The New Problem

Well, really there are two problems: First, the W3Cs current implementation doesn't support same-weight ordering and this creates a new problem of lost expression. This is probably the biggest problem as I will explain, but bad outcomes can be exacerbated by the fact that voters are probably not fully aware of what their vote actually means in terms of outcomes. So, let's talk about why this matters...

In this election, for example I have a very strong preference for both Andrew and David. If you asked me "how much do you support them in this particular election" the answer is "fully". Another candidate's (Reto) statement makes me actively worry that adding them to the TAG would potentially undo some of what I see as valuable progress. I'm sure his work is excellent, but for this reason, if you asked how much I support him in this particular election, the answer is "I don't". The fourth candidate (Lukasz) is somewhere in between those two postions. I would be sad to see either David or Andrew not reelected, but I don't inherently worry that it might derail. In fact, I just don't know a lot about them compared to our current sitting members so in this particular election, all I can say really is I support them "considerably less than fully, but more than 0".

In my opinion, my intentions are clear: I would very much prefer to see both Andrew and David re-elected. It's pretty hard to choose between them, they are both very strong. If pressed, sure, I could choose one (it might vary depending on the election) but the delta of support is small. The delta of my support between then and Lukasz is much larger. However, currently, the W3C system forces me to place them in 1,2,3 order. In this system, in this election, the quota means that in each pass, all you have to do is pass the threshhold to win the seat.

Imagine if 65% of people agreed with my 1,2,3 and 'no other candidate' order, but 35% of people felt precisely the opposite. Those 35% of people think TAG is going in a really bad direction and so they prefer Reto as their first choice and, in order to block anyone who is currently sitting, they cast their second for Lukaz. The result: 35% of first choice votes puts Reto over the threshold and it's a fight to see which of the two candidates that 65% of people intended to get both seats actually gets the other. Which actually received the most 'first votes' could be affected significantly by something as simple as the fact that candidate statements are presented in a fixed order.

Without 'same rank' ability, this system effectively heavily favors the expression supplied by a minority of very passionate supporters by preventing accurate recording of intended preferences.

So what can we do?

Without the ability to express 'same rank' I think the best thing to do is to turn out the vote in a big, big way that prevents an automatic first round win for a least favorite candidate and transfers enough remaining votes to make the difference. We need to make sure that people use the "no other candidate" option to express 0 support if that's what they really mean, otherwise, votes can wind up transferring. There's an argument to be made that the distribution of 'first votes' between the two preferred candidates matters too and it's been suggested that the best way to do this is for each voter who supports two 'first choice' candidates roughly equally to flip a coin to determine which gets first and which gets second. I know, I can't believe that I just wrote that either. However, it can make a differnce and so, it's worth knowing. That said, what this seems to impact most is which of our two preferences winds up with the "other" seat.

The most important thing is to help make sure that ACs know about this effect and how to deal with it. If you're an AC who hasn't voted in the past, please, please vote. If you know an AC (even if you are one yourself), please share this with them. If you are anyone else -- please share it on social media. Believe it or not, we're all wildly connected and you'd be suprised at how easy it is to bump into a connection who either is an AC or at least knows someone who knows one. Just, basically, help get the word out.

Finally, if this seems bad to you, let's stress the importance of getting this fixed.
